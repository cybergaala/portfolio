I have a graveyard of side projects. An app to track what I read. A CLI tool for managing environment variables. A Telegram bot that died after two weeks. Three different portfolio sites before this one.

Most developers have one. The question isn't why projects die — it's what the surviving ones have in common.

## Why I start them

Rarely because I need the thing. Usually because I want to learn something, or because a problem annoyed me enough that solving it feels satisfying even if nobody uses the solution.

The read-tracker died because I didn't actually care about tracking what I read. I cared about learning SwiftData. Once I understood SwiftData, the app had served its purpose.

This is fine. Side projects as learning vehicles are legitimate. The mistake is expecting them to become products.

## The ones that survive

Looking at the projects I've kept running past six months, they share three traits.

**I use them.** Not as a user researcher — as someone who actually needs the thing. The environment variable tool is still running two years later because I open it every workday. I care about it because I depend on it.

**They're small enough to maintain alone.** Anything that requires sustained effort I can't give evenings and weekends becomes a source of guilt rather than enjoyment. The projects I keep are the ones I can ignore for a month and pick back up in twenty minutes.

**They solve one thing.** Scope creep on a side project is the fastest path to abandonment. Every feature I add is maintenance debt I'll owe forever. The projects I'm proud of are the ones where I said no a lot.

## What I've stopped doing

Starting projects with monetization in mind. Every time I've done it, the project feels like a job from day one and I quit within a month.

Building for imaginary users. If I wouldn't use it myself, I probably shouldn't build it.

Optimizing for scale before I have users. One of my projects had three layers of caching before a single person used it. It was overengineering as procrastination — I was building infrastructure instead of shipping.

## The honest take

Most side projects should die. They serve their purpose — a weekend of learning, a problem solved for one person, a proof of concept — and then stop. The goal isn't to keep them alive. The goal is to build something, learn from it, and move on without feeling like you failed.

The graveyard isn't a failure. It's a record of things I tried.
