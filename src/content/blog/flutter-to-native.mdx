I spent three years writing Flutter for production apps. I shipped to the App Store and Play Store, scaled to hundreds of thousands of users, and genuinely enjoyed it. Then we rewrote one of those apps in Swift and Kotlin, and I haven't looked back.

This isn't a "Flutter is bad" post. It's about what happens when cross-platform stops being an advantage.

## The promise

Flutter's pitch is real: one codebase, two platforms, fast iteration. For a small team or early product, that's enormous. You write a widget once. You share your business logic. Your designer hands you a Figma file and you build it exactly, pixel-perfect, without fighting with UIKit auto-layout or Android XML.

For our first two years, this was genuinely true.

## Where it breaks

The problems aren't dramatic. Flutter doesn't crash. The framework isn't broken. What happens is subtler: the gap between what users expect and what you can deliver starts to widen.

On iOS, things like keyboard avoidance, share sheets, haptics, and scroll physics all *work* in Flutter — but they don't feel right. They're close enough that most users won't complain, but they notice. You notice. Every native control is one more wrapper to maintain.

On Android, the situation was worse for us. We use a lot of background processing and Kotlin coroutines fit that model perfectly. Dart's async model is fine, but interop with platform channels adds friction at every edge.

## The rewrite

We didn't rewrite because Flutter failed. We rewrote because we had the team size and timeline to do it right, and the native apps are genuinely better — faster, smaller, and more aligned with each platform's idioms.

What surprised me most was how much I'd missed Swift. Writing `async/await` in Swift, using SwiftUI previews, integrating with HealthKit without a plugin layer — it reminded me why native development has staying power.

## What I'd do differently

Use Flutter when:
- Your team is small and cross-platform velocity matters more than polish
- Your product is primarily UI and business logic, not deep platform integration
- You're prototyping and want to validate fast

Go native when:
- Platform-specific APIs are core to your product
- You have separate iOS/Android engineers anyway
- You're targeting platform power users who notice the difference

Flutter is a great tool. It just isn't always the right one.
